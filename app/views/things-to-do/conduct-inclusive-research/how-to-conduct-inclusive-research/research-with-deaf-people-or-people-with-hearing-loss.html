Research with Deaf people or people with hearing loss

Ask about and meet people's communication needs

Before the research session you should find out the participants communication preference, for example, if the participant:

  • lipreads
  • would like a British Sign Language (BSL) interpreter
  • has a preference for a particular technology, such as a chat function
  • none of these
Example

Thank you for agreeing to take part in our research. In preparation for the session, please let me know how you prefer to communicate.

Understand the importance of the capital D in Deaf

People who identify as culturally Deaf and are actively engaged with the Deaf community tend to use a capital D.

Sign language is often their 1st language. English may be their 2nd language.

Some Deaf people do not see their deafness as a disability.

Face the participant and speak clearly

Make sure you:

  • are in a well-lit room so they can see your face â€“ not with the light behind you
  • reduce background noise and mute your microphone when you're not speaking
  • have the person's attention before you start talking
  • face them and do not cover your mouth
  • speak at normal volume, without exaggerating your lip movements
  • use your usual gestures and facial expressions to support what you're saying and hearing
  • speak directly to them, even if they have an interpreter
  • use plain language and be to the point
  • check they understand what you say and phrase things a different way if they do not

Have written instructions

Write your research instructions in plain English and ask your participant if they would prefer to read them.

Offer captions for remote research

People who lipread may struggle with this in a remote research session. Offer subtitles during a research session, ideally by using a qualified palantypist (someone who takes verbatim notes, turning speech to text).
