General user research tips

Contributors

Test with a broad range of users

Make sure the way you recruit participants is as inclusive as possible. Test with people with diverse backgrounds, abilities and experiences.

Be aware of users' challenges and assistive technology they may be using.

Example

We tested the NHS website's chickenpox page with people who have a range of skin tones. We also tested it with people with different access needs including users with sight loss, dyslexia, cerebral palsy and a neurological disorder.

Before the research session

Send information and consent forms ahead of the session, so participants have time to review them, process information and clarify any concerns.

Alternatively, read through the consent form at the start of the session and get their verbal consent. (Do not forget to record this.)

Consider going to a more convenient place

Sometimes it's better to go somewhere that's more convenient for the person you're researching with, such as their home, or place of work or learning.

This way they may be able to use their own equipment and software and you can get a better idea of how they would use things in daily life.

Give people more time

Build extra time into the research session:

  • for any technical setup
  • so you can build rapport
  • so you can make sure you understand the participant's experience

Be specific

If you're using a recruiter, be as specific as possible about the types of participants you want. Make sure you're happy with the screener they're going to use.

Common pitfalls include confusing:

  • a learning difficulty and a learning disability - find out what a learning disability is on Mencaps's website
  • speech-to-text (for example, Dragon Naturally Speaking) and text-to-speech software (for example, Claro Read)
