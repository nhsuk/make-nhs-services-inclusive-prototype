Research with people with physical or motor disabilities

Contributors

Adapt co-creation activities

Hands-on activities that involve fine motor skills, like cutting out pictures or text and or making drawings, can be difficult for some users. Make sure there are alternative activities for them to do.

Have clear tasks and indicators of success

You may have to rely on observational data for some participants with a severe disability, for example, someone who uses eye-tracking technology to communicate.

Setting clear tasks and indicators will help you evaluate whether they successfully completed tasks.

Ask closed questions

Usually user researchers ask lots of open questions. It's easier for participants who are non-verbal and rely on a communication device, if you design your research to include more closed questions and rely more on observational data.

This is not to say you should not ask any open questions, but you may have to adjust the balance.
