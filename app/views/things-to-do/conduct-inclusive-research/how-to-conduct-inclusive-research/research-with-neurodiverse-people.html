Research with neurodiverse people

Contributors

Reduce anxiety

Some neurodiverse people get very anxious about new situations. They may need to ask questions before the session or want more information in advance than other participants.

They may also need to ask questions at the start of the session and during it. You should allow time for this.

They may feel anxious if they're not comfortable in their surroundings. Take time to check if they need anything to feel more comfortable.

Adapt the way you talk and listen

Ask questions slowly and clearly (without sounding condescending) and give the participant a chance to ask you to clarify things.

Some neurodiverse people may find it difficult to understand what you're asking them to do. If so, reword your questions or find an analogy to help them understand.

They may also find it difficult to express themselves in words. If this happens, watch their body language. Look for signs of focus, interest or frustration. Use observational data like this to help you understand how well your service works for them.

Do not take body language, such as lack of eye contact, negatively. Lack of eye contact does not mean that the person is bored or uninterested.

Consider your language

Do not ask open-ended, vague or hypothetical questions.

Some neurodiverse people find idioms or metaphors difficult to understand, so be clear and explicit.

Break up questions and tasks

Ask 1 question at a time. If you have follow-up questions, ask them after the participant has finished answering your 1st question.

Some people become easily overwhelmed or find it difficult to remember large chunks of information. Split up tasks and questions into smaller, more manageable chunks.

Keep questions and tasks simple and short, and order them logically.

Consider including visuals in instructions

Ask participants if visual as well as written instructions would help. Many neurodiverse people prefer visual prompts to help them understand.

Allow participants to complete a task the way they usually would

It's common in user research to ask participants to imagine a scenario or do a task as a fictional person.

Some neurodiverse people find abstract thinking difficult. Let them complete tasks as themselves in their own way.

Give them more time if need be, so they do not feel under pressure.

Talk about ratings

Some people with cognitive difference may struggle to understand how to give something a rating, for example on a scale of 1 to 5.

If the participant is uncertain, ask them why they gave that rating so they can tell you more about what they're thinking.
