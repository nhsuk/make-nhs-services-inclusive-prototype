Research with neurodiverse people

Contributors

Reduce anxiety

Some neurodiverse people get very anxious about new situations. They may want more information in advance than other participants.

If they need it, allow time at the start of the session for them to ask any questions they need to.

Adapt the way you talk and listen

Some neurodiverse people may find it difficult to understand what you're asking them to do. They may also find it difficult to express themselves in words.

If this happens, watch their body language. Look for signs of focus, interest or frustration.

Consider your language

Avoid open-ended, vague or hypothetical questions.

Some neurodiverse people find idioms or metaphors difficult to understand, so be clear and explicit.

Break up questions and tasks

Some people become easily overwhelmed or find it difficult to remember large chunks of information. Split up tasks and questions into smaller, more manageable chunks.

Keep questions and tasks simple and short, and order them logically.

Include visuals in instructions

Giving people visual as well as written instructions makes it easier for people with dyslexia or learning and communication difficulties to follow.

Allow participants to complete a task the way they usually would

It's common in user research to ask participants to imagine a scenario or do a task as a fictional person.

Some neurodiverse people find abstract thinking difficult. Let them complete tasks as themselves in their own way.

Have a conversation about ratings

People with some cognitive differences may struggle to understand how to answer a question with a rating, for example on a scale of 1 to 5.

If the participant is uncertain, ask them why they gave that rating so they can tell you more about what they're thinking.
